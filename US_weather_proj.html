Project Overview

This project analyzes projected winter temperature changes across the continental United States between 2071 and 2090 using downscaled climate model data. The goal was to understand regional warming patterns and identify areas potentially vulnerable to climate related impacts on ecosystems, infrastructure, and human activities.

Methodology

  • Data:Downscaled projections from global climate models (GCMs) for winter months (Dec–Feb)
  • Software: QGIS for spatial visualization, R for data processing, and Python for raster calculations
  • Analysis steps:
    1. Imported climate projection raster datasets into QGIS
    2. Calculated average winter temperatures for each U.S. region
    3. Generated anomaly maps comparing projected winter temperatures to historical averages
    4. Applied spatial interpolation to smooth results and visualize regional patterns
  • Visualization: Choropleth maps highlighting areas of significant warming, with color gradients representing temperature increases

Key Insights

  • Northern regions of the U.S. are projected to experience the largest increases in winter temperatures
  • Mountainous areas show smaller temperature increases due to elevation effects
  • Urban heat islands may exacerbate warming trends in metropolitan areas, even in winter months
  • Regional temperature patterns highlight potential impacts on agriculture, energy demand, and human comfort
